    These frogs aren\u2019t actually angry or sad, it\u2019s just what these black rain frogs look like<\/h2>\n

    <\/p>\n

    \n
    \n
    <\/div>\n<\/div>\n<\/div>\n

     <\/p>\n

    Native to the Southern coast of Africa, found at elevations of over 3,300ft, often found burrowing to create tunnels up to 6 inches deep.<\/h3>\n
    \n
    <\/div>\n
    \n

    This is especially impressive considering these frogs are tiny, stretching up to a max of 2 inches long.<\/h3>\n

    <\/p>\n

    Since they are a burrowing species who inhabit forest fringes, this frog never actually needs to be in the presence of open water to survive.<\/h3>\n

    <\/p>\n

    When threatened, the black rain frog will puff up, expanding its body to adopt a more aggressive stance.<\/h3>\n
    \n
    <\/div>\n
    \n

    It will often do this while burrowing as the increased size will mean whatever is grabbing it will struggle to pull it out of its hole.<\/h3>\n

    <\/p>\n

    During mating season, the female of the species will discharge a sticky substance to keep the male from falling off its back.<\/h3>\n

    <\/p>\n

    This is called adhesive amplexus, it literally refuses to let go of its mate, now this is what you call loyal.<\/h3>\n
    \n
    <\/div>\n
    \n

    Once the female has laid its eggs, the male will stay within the burrow to guard the eggs and let out little chirps.<\/h3>\n
